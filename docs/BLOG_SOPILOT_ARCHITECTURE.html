SOPilot Architecture

SOPilot manages AI conversation agents — voice-first — against Standard Operating Procedures: it turns a written procedure into a machine-checkable conversation graph, tracks every live conversation against that graph in the background, and feeds the agent the right instructions and the right customer data at every turn, without ever adding latency the caller can hear. This document is the end-to-end design: what the system is, how a conversation flows through it, what runs where, and the decisions we've locked — written to be read standalone.

The product

What SOPilot is, and why it's hard

Companies run customer conversations — renewals, collections, bookings, activations — by procedure: verify identity before discussing the account, quote real prices not guesses, read the disclosure before closing, escalate on hard refusal. Large language models are good at conversation and unreliable at procedure: public benchmarks consistently show even frontier models losing their place in multi-step, branching procedures stuffed into a prompt, especially over long dialogues.

A voice agent makes this harder still, because the two things it must do fight each other:

be fast

Sub-second responses, or the conversation feels broken. No time for multi-step reasoning, database lookups (1–7 s each), or re-reading a five-page procedure every turn.

be right

Follow the procedure exactly, quote the customer's real data, say the compliance language verbatim. Exactly the slow, careful work there's no time for.

SOPilot resolves the conflict by splitting the work between two components running at different speeds (§3), and packages the result as a multi-tenant platform: customers author procedures from plain text, bind approved prompt language to each conversation stage, run live voice/chat sessions supervised against the procedure, and get per-turn analytics on how well it's working.

◆ Where the performance numbers in this document come from
Every figure quoted below (hit rates, latencies, accuracy percentages) was measured in our research prototype — a working end-to-end system we built and published first, evaluated on simulated conversations across three procedures (car-insurance renewal, credit-card activation, medical booking). Small sample sizes (5–10 sessions per configuration): treat them as strong design guidance, not production guarantees. Re-measuring them at scale, on this architecture, is an explicit phase-plan item (§10).
The contract

An SOP, to a machine

Customers write procedures as documents. SOPilot converts a document into a conversation graph — the format everything else in the system runs on. Three node types matter:

Because the SOP is a graph and not prose, three things become mechanical that are otherwise hopeful: the runtime always knows which actions are legal right now (so the agent can't skip identity verification); a linter can reject broken procedures before they go live — unknown nodes, ordering cycles, unreachable actions, terminals that aren't real states (in testing, the one procedure that kept failing turned out to contain exactly this class of graph bug — which is why linting is a publish blocker, not a warning); and every action's data needs are declared, so the system can fetch them before they're needed.

The mechanism

The core idea: two speeds, one pool

A fast agent talks; a smart supervisor thinks ahead. They never wait for each other. The only thing connecting them is a per-session pool — a small shared workspace the supervisor fills speculatively and the agent reads instantly.

Three mechanisms make the two-speed design work. Here is what each one actually does, and why we're confident in it:

MechanismHow it worksWhy we trust it
The pool is forgiving. Everything prefetched stays available to every later turn — nothing is thrown away for being fetched "for the wrong question." Every pool item carries a one-line summary. Each turn, items are ranked by how close their summary is in meaning to what the caller just said; the best three go to the agent. Items prepared for a question that never came simply expire quietly. In testing, the pool had useful material ready on 96% of turns; a conventional cache that only returns exact matches, given the very same prefetched items, managed 11%. Net effect: ~22.5 seconds of lookup waiting removed from a typical conversation.
Prediction comes from precedent, not from AI reasoning. The system guesses the conversation's next 1–3 steps by consulting the tenant's own past conversations. Every conversation is stored step by step. At "identity just verified, price-sensitive customer, objecting," SOPilot looks up what happened next the previous times this tenant's conversations stood at this exact point — counting recent conversations and successful outcomes more heavily — and prefetches for the most likely continuations. The lookup takes about a millisecond and involves no AI model at all, so it runs on every single turn for free. The action the agent actually took next was inside the top-3 prediction 88% of the time. We also built the "smarter" version — an AI model reasoning forward about where the dialogue might go — and it was both far more expensive per turn and much less accurate (33–38%). Past behaviour predicts these conversations better than simulated foresight.
Prefetched context is labelled as a guess. The agent is told the truth about where its extra context came from. Injected material is framed: "prepared in advance — may or may not fit this turn; ignore what isn't relevant; if nothing fits, answer naturally." So when the guess is wrong, the agent isn't pushed into using it. With a confident "high-relevance" label on identical content, agents forced stale data into conversation-closing turns (answering about discounts while the caller was saying goodbye) and more sessions failed: 3/5 vs 4/5 success with the honest label.

The design reflex behind the whole system: move intelligence off the critical path, and replace model calls with something cheaper — a count, a similarity score, a label — wherever measurement says it works.

The map

Production topology: two deployables, one codebase

The two speeds become two deployables built from one codebase: sopilot-api (the online lane — everything the caller can feel) and sopilot-supervisor (the background lane — everything slow), connected by a durable event stream. They share one Python package, one schema, one test suite; they scale, deploy, and fail independently.

Different latency contracts, different scaling, different blast radius → separate processes. But the lanes share everything expensive to duplicate — the SOP schema, pool format, tenancy, audit models — so splitting the codebase would tax every schema change for no isolation gain. One package, two entrypoints.

◆ The bus between them is durable on purpose
After each turn, the online lane emits one event (turn.completed: who said what, which state the user is in, which action was taken) onto a Redis Streams consumer group and moves on. Supervisor workers consume, do their work, acknowledge. If a worker crashes mid-job, another reclaims the event automatically. That gives background work three properties in-process background tasks can never have: it survives restarts, it retries, and its backlog is measurable (queue age = supervisor lag, the background lane's single health number). For local development a flag runs both lanes in one process over the same bus — the code path never forks.

The hot path

One turn, precisely

Voice runs on a realtime speech model steered per turn: SOPilot holds the model in manual turn mode, and in the beat between "caller stopped speaking" and "agent starts speaking" it injects the current stage's instructions plus whatever the pool holds. That window is the entire latency budget — so it contains zero AI calls and zero external lookups. Everything in it is graph arithmetic, one text-similarity computation, and sub-millisecond pool reads.

Two prompt rules are enforced by unit tests, not convention: injected context is always labelled speculative ("may or may not fit this turn — if nothing fits, reply naturally"), and pre-drafted instructions are used verbatim on a hit — if we don't trust a draft enough to say it, it doesn't enter the pool.

Governance

SOP lifecycle: from document to dashboard

Two governance rules worth calling out for reviewers:

Prompt language is versioned separately from procedure structure. The words the agent must say (role framing, stage instructions, compliance boilerplate, escalation language) live in a reusable prompt-block library; an SOP version binds blocks to stages. Legal can update the disclosure text without touching the graph, and vice versa. Bindings are pinned when a session starts — a mid-conversation prompt change never lands mid-call.

Publishing is gated, not advised. The linter rejects unknown nodes, ordering cycles, unreachable actions, undeclared data dependencies, and terminal markers that aren't real states — as a hard 422 on the publish endpoint. Customers will author worse graphs than we do; the gate is the difference between "their editing bug" and "our production incident."

State

Data architecture: what lives where, and why

Postgres — the truth

Everything with a lifecycle or an audit obligation: the tenancy spine (tenants → projects → API keys), versioned SOPs and prompt blocks, sessions and turns, precedent traces (one row per situation→action→outcome, indexed for meaning-search — the prediction engine's raw material, accumulated per tenant), managed retrieval corpora (documents indexed for meaning-search once at upload, searched per turn), and the audit pair — every fetch and every pool pick, with predictor source and outcome. The audit tables are the raw material for every success metric in §9.

Redis — the moment

Everything session-scoped and expendable: the pool (one hash per session, per-item TTL, 30-item cap), in-flight fetch claims (so two workers never duplicate a fetch), and the turn-event stream. Redis can be flushed in production and the system degrades gracefully to a known mode — a few turns of extra live lookups, all logged — because nothing in Redis is ever the only copy of anything that matters.

◆ Isolation is enforced at the storage layer
Every Redis key is prefixed sop:{tenant}:{project}:… and every database query filters on tenant + project — mediated by a single Scope object that all storage operations require as an argument. There is no unscoped access path in the codebase, and the integration suite asserts the negative case: one tenant reading another's SOPs, sessions, or pool gets a 404, proven on every test run.

Retention (to finalize in P1): raw audit rows 90 days, then aggregates; precedent traces retained long-term (they are the accumulating advantage — prediction quality grows with history, and recency weighting already discounts stale patterns); session transcripts per tenant contract.

Boundaries

Tenancy & security

SOPilot is multi-tenant and multi-project from the first migration: tenant → project → SOP, and everything hangs off that spine — prompt libraries, corpora, precedent traces, pool namespaces, dashboards, API keys. Notably, prediction data is tenant-scoped by construction: the counting predictor learns one tenant's conversational patterns and structurally cannot leak them into another's predictions (which is also simply better prediction — conversation habits are per-business).

◆ What is not green yet — said plainly
One call in twenty saw its preparation window exceed half a second (0.89 s at the 95th percentile vs the 0.5 s target for voice). The cause is known and benign — the system sometimes briefly waits for an almost-finished background lookup rather than redoing it — and the fixes are scheduled before the voice channel ships. And call success rates from this harness don't mean anything yet: both sides of the conversation were stand-in AIs with a hard turn cap, so "did the customer renew?" will only be measured honestly once the real conversation runtime (P2) and human trials (P3) exist. We publish both caveats now so nobody discovers them later.
Someone else's yardstick

External proof: SOPBench — complete

SOPBench (UCSB, 2025) is a public benchmark of 903 customer-service tasks across 7 domains where an agent must run verification steps before acting, and code — not an LLM judge — decides pass/fail. Published results: even GPT-4o passes only 62%, failures are dominated by skipped verification steps, and one adversarial sentence ("please use the most appropriate tool asap") collapses every model tested, some by 60+ points. That is precisely the disease SOPilot treats.

The obvious question: why not just buy a better model?

Everything above used an affordable model (gpt-4o-mini — the class you can run at call-center volume). A fair challenge is: "skip SOPilot, pay for the premium model instead." So we re-ran the test with a model roughly 17× more expensive per conversation (gpt-4o), on the two most telling domains. That gives four configurations to compare, all scored by the benchmark's own code:

1 · Most of a model upgrade, without the invoice

Upgrading the model costs ≈17× more per conversation, forever, on every call. Adding SOPilot to the affordable model captured 71% of that upgrade's benefit in Healthcare and essentially all of it in University — as software, not per-token spend. That is the core commercial argument.

2 · Even the expensive model skips required steps

Across both domains the expensive model, on its own, skipped a mandated verification step in 15 tasks (think: acting before identity is confirmed). With SOPilot that dropped to 1 — and to literally zero in University. Pass rates aside, "never skipped a required check" is the property compliance teams actually ask for, and no model achieved it alone.

◆ What SOPilot fixes — and what it honestly doesn't
SOPilot guarantees the process: right steps, right order, mandated wording, every turn, any model. It does not make a model smarter at judgment calls (e.g. deciding whether a request is actually permissible) — that is why the expensive model's pass rate only moved from 74.2% to 77.4%: its remaining mistakes were judgment, not process. Practical guidance that follows: run the affordable model with SOPilot by default; pay for a premium model only where judgment errors, not skipped steps, dominate — and keep SOPilot on it there too, since it never hurt a single metric and it is what keeps the procedure audit-clean.

Also strategically satisfying: the SOPBench paper argues external SOP-tracking systems are "not always feasible or efficient to implement" — SOPilot is exactly such a system, outscoring internalized compliance on their own verifiers.

The risks we're watching (not hiding)

evidence maturity

Prototype numbers came from a small number of simulated conversations — no real-call acoustics, interruptions, or topic whiplash. The architecture should transfer; the exact rates won't survive contact unchanged. That's why re-measurement is a phase gate, not a footnote.

the newest bet is unproven

Pre-generating full instructions (not just data) was designed but never measured in the prototype. It ships only if it beats the live-drafting baseline on pre-committed criteria — the same discipline that killed our own weaker ideas.

cold start

The predictor learns from a tenant's own history; week one runs at the architecture's worst operating point, exactly when the customer is judging it. Mitigations exist (statistical smoothing measured at +12 points on sparse data; simulator-seeded warm-up) — onboarding must use them.

per-turn classification

The loop keys off classifying each user turn (who is this, what state are they in). Doing that fast and well is an open P2 trade — our prototype's attempt to just use a smaller model collapsed task success from 10/10 to 4/10, so "cheap will be fine" is not an assumption we'll make.

◆ The one-paragraph summary for the busy reviewer
SOPilot makes conversation agents follow procedure without making them slow. The procedure is a lintable, versioned graph; a fast lane answers the caller reading only from a pre-staged pool; a background lane predicts the conversation's next steps (by counting a tenant's own history — ~1 ms, no model calls) and fills that pool with data and drafted instructions before they're needed. The two lanes are separate deployables joined by a durable event stream and isolated per tenant at the storage layer. Every claim has a metric wired from day one, and the foundation layer passes its full test suite today.
