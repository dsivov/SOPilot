Scope

Objectives & the data

The dataset is 4,227 cleaned, diarized (Client / Agent) transcripts of the Málaga airport information desk, 17 April – 15 July 2026, with per-conversation timestamp, duration, language, and full turn text. This analysis asks six questions of it: what travellers ask, when they call, how long/complex the exchanges are, who talks, whether topics form data-driven clusters, and which conversation properties move together.

Timing

Temporal patterns

Peak hour is 10:00 local; the busiest days are Sun, Fri, Sat — consistent with weekend and Friday leisure-travel peaks. Staffing and agent readiness should track this shape.

Distributions

Conversation shape

Both distributions are strongly right-skewed: most exchanges are short (a single question and answer), with a long tail of extended conversations. Duration — min 3s, median 49s, p90 124s, max 368s. Turns — median 7, p90 15, max 47. The typical interaction is transactional; the tail is where confusion, language barriers, or multi-part requests live.

Agents speak a minority of the words (39% at the median): the desk pattern is a traveller explaining a need at some length, met by a short, directive answer (a desk, a belt, a direction). Agent utterances are shorter on average than client utterances — 17 vs 23 words — but fewer in aggregate, which is the signature of concise, high-information responses.

Duration and turn count move together moderately (r = 0.608): longer calls have more exchanges, but not linearly — a slow single question can run as long as a brisk multi-part one. Language and topic are significantly associated (χ² = 536, p ≈ 1e-103) but the effect is modest (Cramér's V = 0.252): the topic mix differs by language (e.g. the balance of luggage vs transport questions) without any language being single-topic.

What's in the uncovered demand

The next candidate topics

Beyond the four dominant themes, 1,252 conversations (30%) are not one of them. Clustering that pool within-language and labelling each group separates genuine service needs from noise (greetings, closings, chit-chat, unintelligible transcripts). About 52% of the pool is actionable demand; the rest is non-topic noise — itself a useful finding.

Segmentation

Cohorts

Most conversations are quick (≤4 turns); the extended (>10 turn) cohort is the minority but consumes disproportionate desk time and is where the long-tail languages and multi-part requests concentrate. Segmenting by day-part confirms the morning-to-afternoon load the hourly chart shows.

Takeaways

Findings that inform future work

  1. Demand is concentrated and directional. Four themes cover most traffic and nearly every answer ends in wayfinding — the corpus is fundamentally about routing people to places.
  2. Interactions are short and transactional (median 49s, 7 turns) with a heavy tail; the median exchange is one question and one directive answer.
  3. Load is predictable — peaks at 10:00 and on weekends/Fridays — so capacity (human or automated) can be planned against a known shape.
  4. Language is the strongest axis in the raw signal, and a real multilingual long tail (French, Welsh, Malay, Italian) exists beyond the Spanish/English majority — quantified here for the first time.
  5. Topic ≠ tight clusters. Unsupervised clustering is language-dominated and topically soft; a curated lexicon labels topics more reliably, and the two agree where it matters. Any future topic model should either work within a language or normalise language first.
  6. Duration is only moderately explained by turn count (r=0.608); call length is driven as much by pacing/confusion as by the number of exchanges — a lever worth watching in any service metric.
