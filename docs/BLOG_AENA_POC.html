The raw material

The data we received

6,262 transcript files covering 90 days of the Málaga airport information desk (17 April – 15 July 2026). Each file is one conversation, already diarized into Client and Agent lines by the recording system, with start/end timestamps in the filename.

37 s

median conversation (p95: 2½ minutes). These are short, transactional exchanges — one question, one answer, goodbye. Exactly the shape a voice agent must serve fast.

10 turns

median dialogue length before cleaning (mean 12). After echo removal and merging, real exchanges are typically 4–8 genuine turns.

3 languages

Spanish ~44%, English ~35%, the rest too short to classify — with a visible French minority inside it. The POC must be multilingual from day one.

◆ Data handling rule — absolute
These are real conversations with real travellers. The corpus lives only on our analysis machine, is excluded from version control at the ignore level (scripts and aggregate numbers are the only things committed), and this document quotes no conversation verbatim. Before any external LLM touches the content (SOP mining), we need a PII-masking pass and the customer's explicit sign-off — see §7.
Garbage taxonomy

What's wrong with it — the four defects

This is machine-transcribed speech from a noisy terminal hall. Four defect classes, in order of impact:

DefectWhat it looks likeHow commonOur handling
Empty / trivial files recorder triggered, nobody really spoke — zero to two lines of greeting noise 2,035 files (32%)drop
Diarization echo the same utterance captured twice within seconds — once under each speaker label (two microphones hearing one voice). Illustrative shape: Agent: "you need to ask the airline" → Client: "you need to ask the airline" 5,197 turns; up to 75% of turns in the worst filesrepair — keep first occurrence, drop near-duplicates (containment counts for long lines)
Speaker mislabeling runs of agent speech filed under Client (and vice versa) — the answer text sits on the asker's label frequent in long filesmitigate — echo removal fixes the doubled cases; analysis weights content, not labels; full re-diarization is out of scope
ASR mangling proper nouns and desk names garbled (a baggage-handler brand rendered as a made-up word, place names drifting between repetitions) pervasive but tolerablekeep — meaning usually survives; matters only for verbatim prompt mining, where we'll use the cleanest examples
The pipeline

Cleaning: 6,262 files → 4,227 dialogues

The pipeline (use_case/analysis/clean.py) emits one JSON record per dialogue — speaker-tagged turns, language, duration, echo statistics — which every later step consumes. Nothing irreversible happens to the raw files; the pipeline is re-runnable as we tighten heuristics.

◆ The lost-luggage insight
Lost-luggage calls are the clearest procedure in the corpus: agents consistently walk the same decision tree (which airline → is it airline-handled or ground-handler → send to the right desk with directions → mention the reference code they'll need). The proposal

The POC we propose: Málaga Airport Information Agent

A voice-first agent covering the top four themes = 71% of real traffic, built as one SOPilot tenant (aena) with three mined SOPs:

SOP 1

Lost-luggage triage

The clearest procedure in the data: airline → handler desk routing, directions, reference-code guidance. High emotion, high value — the case where following the procedure calms the caller.

SOP 2

Flight & boarding info

29% of traffic. Needs one real data connector (flight-status lookup) — a perfect showcase for background retrieval: the answer is pre-fetched while the caller finishes saying the flight number.

SOP 3

Transport, parking & wayfinding

31% combined. Mostly static knowledge (stops, desks, floors) — a RAG corpus of airport facts serves it; every answer ends in directions, matching the co-occurrence pattern.

The three procedures, as machines see them

These are not sketches — they are the live conversation graphs the runtime executes, generated from the published SOP definitions. Purple stages are what the agent does (in enforced order); blue states are what the classifier hears from the traveller; green/red states end the conversation.

SOP 1 — Lost & delayed luggage the emotional one

Mined from the lost-luggage conversations. The procedure the human agents demonstrably follow: find out the airline (the decision point — each airline has a different handling company), check where the bag was last seen, then route to the right desk with physical directions — Ground Force, Avia Partner or Menzies, each at a specific belt. Every stage from greeting onward carries the airport-facts connector (amber dots), so desk locations come from the mined fact base, not the model's imagination. Conversations end Resolved (answered on the spot), Routed (sent to the right desk — the normal outcome here), or CallerGivesUp.

SOP 2 — Flight & boarding info 29% of all traffic

The highest-volume theme. The mined flow identifies the flight or airline, then answers: which counters, when check-in opens (the "two hours before departure" rule appears in dozens of conversations), gate and terminal directions. Two connectors attach here: airport-facts for counters and rules, and flight-status — mock today, AENA's live flight API when provided. This is the SOP where background retrieval shines: the counter answer is pre-fetched while the traveller is still finishing their sentence.

SOP 3 — Transport, parking & wayfinding 31% combined

Buses, trains, taxis, parking and "where is…" questions — the theme where the v1 replay already scores best (only 2 of 12 missed). The flow forks after the answer: simple questions end at ProvideTransportInfo; physical services (taxi rank, train station, parking payment) continue to RouteToService with walking directions. One connector (airport-facts) serves the whole procedure.

Why this is a strong POC for AENA specifically: the calls are short and repetitive (median 37 s — ideal for latency-hidden retrieval and pre-drafted replies), trilingual (the realtime voice channel handles language switching), and the evaluation writes itself — replay the held-out real questions at the agent and measure answer coverage against what human agents did, using the Autopilot harness we already built.

◆ What the remaining gap actually is — the finding that matters
Reading every "partial" verdict, one pattern dominates: the human agent's answer was operationally dynamic — "check-in at counter 343", "counter 355, two hours before" — that flight's counter, that day, read off the airport's live screens. A static fact base can never hold these; the same airline is at a different counter tomorrow. This is precisely the flight-status / counter-assignment connector the architecture reserved a slot for (mock today). The concrete ask to AENA: access to the live counter-assignment and flight-status feed — with it, the dominant class of "partial" becomes "covered" mechanically, because the pipeline that serves facts into answers is already proven live.

Remaining static-knowledge issues are smaller: a few desk locations are reported variably across the corpus (Ground Force appears at belt 36, 38, and behind La Manon in different months — likely all true at different times), which only AENA's canonical floor data resolves; and the replay method still under-scores clarifying-question flows (the recorded traveller answers the human agent's flow, not the AI's).

Method notes (v1 detail)

36 held-out real conversations (never seen by mining) replayed through the live POC; a judge compared the AI's answers with what the human agent actually provided:

ThemeCoveredPartialMissed
transport & wayfinding3/1272

total7/36 (19%)12 (33%)17 (47%)

Why v1 scored low — the three causes iteration one addressed: (1) the fact base has 25 facts while the human agents demonstrate a long tail of desk knowledge (per-airline handler desks, per-flight belts) — the miss notes are dominated by "AI missed the desk location"; (2) the mined SOPs verify before answering, while real desk agents answer first and verify only when routing requires it; (3) replay desynchronization — when the AI asks a clarifying question, the recorded traveller's next line answers the human agent's different flow, which the judge scores as a miss. Iteration one: mine facts from ALL agent answers (not just the SOP-mining sample), make the SOPs answer-first, neutral retrieval queries. The harness re-runs in minutes, so this number is a baseline to climb, not a verdict.
